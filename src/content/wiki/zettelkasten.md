A *Zettelkasten* ("slip box" in German) is a method of personal knowledge
management built from many small, linked notes. It was made famous by the
sociologist Niklas Luhmann, who used a paper card system of around 90,000 notes
to write more than 70 books and hundreds of articles.

## How it works

The system rests on a few simple moves:

- **Atomic notes.** Each note holds exactly one idea, written in your own words.
  One idea per card keeps notes reusable in many contexts.
- **Unique identifiers.** Every note gets a stable ID so it can be referenced
  forever, even as the collection grows.
- **Links over folders.** Instead of filing notes into a single category, you
  connect them to related notes. A note can live in many "places" at once
  through its links.
- **An index.** A handful of entry-point notes point into clusters of the box,
  so you can find your way back in.

## Why it works

The power is not in storage but in *surprise*. When you add a new note, you are
forced to ask where it connects. Those connections put old thoughts back in
front of you, and unexpected pairs of ideas become the seeds of new writing.
Luhmann described the box as a conversation partner — something he could "talk
to" and be surprised by.

## Starting small

You do not need 90,000 cards. Begin with ten notes on something you actually
care about, and link each new note to at least one existing one. The habit of
linking matters far more than the size of the box.
