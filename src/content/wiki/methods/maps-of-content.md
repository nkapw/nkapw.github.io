A **Map of Content** (MOC) is a note whose only job is to link to other notes
around a shared theme. The term comes from the
[Linking Your Thinking](/wiki/linking-your-thinking/) approach, where MOCs act
as flexible hubs you navigate through — like the index of a city you are still
building.

This page lives at `wiki/methods/maps-of-content`, one folder deep, so the
breadcrumb above reads **Home / Methods / Maps of Content**. The trail is built
from the folder structure, not hand-written.

## What a MOC is not

A MOC is not a rigid table of contents fixed in advance. It is grown
*bottom-up*: you write atomic notes first, notice a cluster forming, then make a
MOC to gather them. Structure emerges from use.

## How to build one

1. Keep writing small, single-idea notes.
2. When several notes circle the same theme, create a note titled after that
   theme.
3. List links to the related notes, with a sentence each on *why* they belong.
4. Revisit the MOC as the cluster grows — promote, split, or merge as needed.

## Why it helps

Folders force a note into one location. A MOC lets the same note belong to many
themes at once through links, and it records *why* the notes relate — something
a folder name never can. The MOC becomes the map you reach for when you re-enter
a topic after weeks away.

See also [evergreen notes](/wiki/evergreen-notes/) for how to write the
individual notes a MOC points to.
