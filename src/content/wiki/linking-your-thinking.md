*Linking Your Thinking* (often shortened to LYT) is a philosophy of note-making
that treats links — not folders — as the primary way to organize knowledge. The
core claim is that meaning lives in *relationships between ideas*, so a good
note system should make those relationships cheap to create and easy to follow.

## Atomic notes, again

LYT shares the atomic-note idea with the [Zettelkasten](/wiki/zettelkasten/)
method and [evergreen notes](/wiki/evergreen-notes/): keep each note focused on
one idea. But it adds an emphasis on *navigation*. The question is not only "what
does this note say?" but "how do I move through my notes the way I move through
a city?"

## Maps of content

The signature LYT tool is the **Map of Content** (MOC) — a note whose job is to
link to other notes around a theme. A MOC is not a rigid table of contents; it
is a flexible, living hub you grow as a topic develops. Where a folder forces a
note into exactly one location, a note can appear in many MOCs at once.

You typically build MOCs *bottom-up*: write atomic notes first, notice a cluster
forming, then create a MOC to gather them. Structure emerges from use rather
than being imposed in advance.

## Why links beat folders

- A folder is a single, permanent home; a link is a cheap, repeatable
  relationship.
- Links record *why* two ideas belong together, which a folder name cannot.
- Following links surfaces forgotten notes exactly when they are relevant.

## The mindset

LYT asks you to stop *collecting* and start *connecting*. Saving an article is
not thinking. Writing a sentence about how it relates to something you already
believe — that is thinking, and it is what the links capture.
