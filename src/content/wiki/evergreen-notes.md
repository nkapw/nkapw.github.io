*Evergreen notes* are notes you write to evolve, contribute, and accumulate over
time — across projects, not just for one. The term comes from Andy Matuschak,
who contrasts them with the *transient* notes most people take: scribbles in a
meeting, highlights in a book, a to-do that is thrown away once acted on.

## The principles

Matuschak describes a handful of properties that make a note evergreen:

- **Atomic.** Each note is about one concept. Atomic notes are easy to link to
  precisely and easy to recombine.
- **Concept-oriented.** Title the note by the *idea* it expresses, not by the
  book or meeting it came from. "Closing the loop reduces anxiety" is evergreen;
  "Notes from Tuesday's call" is not.
- **Densely linked.** A note earns its keep through its connections. Writing the
  links is the thinking.
- **Written for yourself, then refined.** Start rough, but rewrite the note each
  time you touch it so it states the idea more clearly than before.

## Why bother

Most note-taking is *write once, read never*. Evergreen notes flip this: the
note is a unit you return to and improve, so your understanding compounds.
Because each note is a tidy statement of one idea, drafting an essay becomes a
matter of arranging notes you already wrote rather than starting from a blank
page.

## A practical test

Before saving a note, ask: *would this still make sense, and still be useful, a
year from now with no other context?* If not, rewrite it until it would. That
single question is most of the discipline.
